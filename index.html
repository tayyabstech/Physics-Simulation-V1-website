Fusion vs Fission

How do fusion and fission compare as viable nuclear energy power sources?

Edwin · Jehan · Tayyab · Leon

Move through the slides with the buttons below (or your arrow keys), then experiment with our interactive reactor simulation at the end.

Context

Why this question matters

Electricity demand is rising while the world tries to cut carbon emissions. Today, about 440 fission reactors supply roughly 9% of the world's electricity, the second-largest source of low-carbon power after hydro (IAEA, 2024).

Fission splits heavy nuclei and is a mature, 70-year-old technology. Fusion joins light nuclei, the same process that powers the Sun, and it has never generated a single watt of grid electricity. But in December 2022 the National Ignition Facility achieved ignition for the first time: 2.05 MJ of laser energy in, 3.15 MJ of fusion energy out (LLNL, 2022).

So which process is the more viable power source? To answer that we need the physics: where the energy comes from, what fuels each reaction needs, what each produces, and what can go wrong.

The physics toolkit

Key terms & definitions

Nuclide notation

A nucleus is written A
ZX where A = mass number (protons + neutrons) and Z = atomic number (protons). e.g. 235
92U has 92 protons and 143 neutrons.

Isotopes

Atoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons (different A). Deuterium 2
1H and tritium 3
1H are isotopes of hydrogen.

Strong nuclear force

The short-range attractive force that binds nucleons together, overpowering the electrostatic (Coulomb) repulsion between protons, but only at separations of about 10−15 m.

Binding energy

The energy needed to pull a nucleus apart into separate nucleons. That is also the energy released when the nucleus forms. It is measured in mega-electronvolts (1 MeV = 1.602 × 10−13 J).

Mass defect & E = mc²

A bound nucleus has less mass than its separate nucleons. The missing mass Δm becomes energy: E = Δmc² (Einstein, 1905). Every joule of nuclear energy, fusion or fission, is paid for by mass.

Worked example: helium-4

2p + 2n = 4.03298 u, but a real He-4 nucleus is only 4.00260 u. The missing Δm = 0.03038 u becomes E = 28.3 MeV of binding energy (1 u = 931.5 MeV/c²) (CODATA, 2022).

These terms are set out here in words, symbols and equations. We use them consistently on the slides that follow.

The one graph that explains everything

The binding energy curve

Plot the binding energy per nucleon against mass number and one curve explains both reactions. Nuclei become more stable as you move up the curve, and any reaction that moves nucleons up the curve releases the difference in binding energy.

  • Fusion: light nuclei (H, He) sit low on the steep left slope. Fusing them climbs sharply, releasing up to ~3.5 MeV per nucleon (D–T).
  • Fission: very heavy nuclei (U, Pu) sit past the peak. Splitting them into mid-mass fragments climbs gently back, ~0.85 MeV per nucleon.
  • Iron-56 sits at the peak: it can release energy neither way.

This single relationship is why fusion releases roughly 4× more energy per kilogram of fuel than fission, and why both count as "nuclear energy" despite being opposite processes.

Fusion · Part 1

Fusion in stars: the proton–proton chain

Main-sequence stars like our Sun generate energy by fusing hydrogen into helium through the proton–proton (p–p) chain (Bethe, 1939):

0.7% of the reacting mass is converted to energy (E = Δmc²), the highest fraction of any reaction on these slides.

Reactants, products & conditions

  • Reactants: ordinary hydrogen, the most abundant element in the universe. The Sun converts ~4 × 10⁹ kg of mass to energy every second.
  • Products: helium-4, positrons (which annihilate with electrons, adding energy), neutrinos and gamma rays.
  • Conditions: ~15 million K and immense gravitational pressure. Even then, the first step relies on the weak interaction and on quantum tunnelling through the Coulomb barrier. On average, a given proton waits billions of years before it fuses.

That slowness is why stars burn steadily for billions of years, and why the p–p chain is useless as a reactor design. Earth-based fusion needs a much faster reaction: deuterium–tritium.

Fusion · Part 2

Fusion on Earth: the deuterium–tritium reaction

2
1H + 3
1H → 4
2He (3.5 MeV) + 1
0n (14.1 MeV)

Energy released: 17.6 MeV · 0.38% of mass converted

D–T has the largest reaction probability (cross-section) at the lowest temperature of any fusion reaction, which is why every serious reactor project (ITER, JET, NIF) uses it (ITER Organization, 2025).

By conservation of momentum the lighter product carries most of the kinetic energy: the neutron takes 14.1 MeV, 80% of the yield. Because it is uncharged, it escapes the magnetic field and is captured in the reactor's blanket, where its energy becomes heat.

The Coulomb barrier problem

Both nuclei are positive, so they repel until they are within ~10−15 m, where the strong force takes over. Without a star's pressure, we compensate with temperature above 100–150 million K (~10× the Sun's core). At these temperatures matter is a plasma, confined by magnetic fields (tokamaks) or compressed by lasers (inertial confinement, NIF).

Fusion · Part 3

Fusion fuel: availability and energy density

  • Deuterium: 1 in ~6,420 hydrogen atoms in seawater (IAEA, 2023). The oceans hold enough for millions of years of world energy use, effectively inexhaustible.
  • Tritium: radioactive (half-life 12.3 y), so almost none exists naturally. The entire world inventory is only about 25 kg. It has to be bred from lithium inside the reactor blanket, using the fusion neutron itself:

6
3Li + 1
0n → 4
2He + 3
1H (+4.8 MeV)

1 kg of D–T fuel releases about as much energy as 10,000 tonnes of coal. The catch is that breeding tritium at full reactor scale is still unproven, which is a genuine weakness in fusion's fuel story (we come back to it on slide 12).

Fission · Part 1

Fission of U-235: the chain reaction

Fission splits a heavy nucleus into two mid-mass fragments. A typical U-235 fission:

1
0n + 235
92U → 236
92U* → 141
56Ba + 92
36Kr + 31
0n

Energy released: ~200 MeV per fission · ~0.09% of mass converted

  • Slow (thermal) neutrons (~0.025 eV) trigger U-235 fission far more readily than fast ones; the capture probability is hundreds of times higher. That is why reactors use a moderator (water or graphite) to slow down the fast ~2 MeV neutrons that fission produces.
  • Each fission frees 2–3 new neutrons → a chain reaction. If on average exactly one neutron causes another fission (k = 1) the reactor is critical and steady; k > 1 grows exponentially. Control rods (boron/cadmium) absorb neutrons to hold k = 1.
  • Only 0.72% of natural uranium is U-235, so reactor fuel is enriched to 3–5% (above 90% is weapons-grade).

This split is only one of many possible outcomes; the fragment pairs vary each time. You can try it yourself in the simulation at the end.

Fission · Part 2

Fission products, radiation & waste

  • Asymmetric split: fragments cluster around A ≈ 95 (e.g. Kr-92, Sr-90) and A ≈ 140 (e.g. Ba-141, Cs-137, I-131). About 90% of the 200 MeV appears as fragment kinetic energy, which becomes heat in the fuel.
  • Unstable fragments: fission fragments are neutron-rich, so they decay by β⁻ emission (a neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino), usually with γ radiation; heavy actinides in spent fuel also emit α particles.
  • Decay heat: even after shutdown, the decaying fragments still produce about 7% of full power. This residual heat is what melted the Fukushima cores after the reactors had already been shut down (World Nuclear Association, 2024).

High-level waste must be isolated for ~10,000+ years. Finland's Onkalo repository, the world's first deep geological disposal site, is only now beginning operation (Posiva, 2025).

Fusion's contrast: its direct product (He-4) is stable. Neutron bombardment does make reactor components radioactive, but with half-lives of decades, not millennia.

Fission · Part 3

Neutron absorption in U-238: breeding Pu-239

Most of a fuel rod (95–97%) is U-238, which rarely fissions. Instead it captures a neutron and, through two β⁻ decays, becomes plutonium-239:

238
92U + 1
0n → 239
92U + γ

239
92U → 239
93Np + β⁻ + ν̄ (t½ = 23.5 min)

239
93Np → 239
94Pu + β⁻ + ν̄ (t½ = 2.36 days)

Pu-239 is fissile. Unlike U-235, which needs slow neutrons, Pu-239 fissions efficiently with fast neutrons. This is the basis of fast breeder reactors, which run without a moderator and can create more fuel than they burn.

Why breeding cuts both ways

  • In an ordinary reactor, bred Pu-239 quietly supplies roughly a third of the energy by the end of a fuel cycle (World Nuclear Association, 2024).
  • Breeder reactors could stretch uranium supplies ~60-fold by burning the abundant U-238.
  • The same physics also produces weapons-grade material, since Pu-239 is the core of most nuclear arsenals. This dual-use problem is the reason for the international safeguards regime (the NPT and IAEA inspections), a legal factor we return to on slide 13.

Fusion has no equivalent chain: D–T reactions cannot "run away" (any disturbance cools the plasma and fusion simply stops) and produce no fissile material.

Engineering

From nucleus to grid: the energy transformations

Nuclear potential energy→ Kinetic energy of fragments & neutrons→ Thermal energy in coolant→ Kinetic energy of steam & turbine→ Electrical energy
  • Fragment kinetic energy heats the fuel; pressurised water carries the heat out of the core (the water also acts as the moderator).
  • A heat exchanger boils a separate water loop into steam, keeping radioactive water contained.
  • Steam spins a turbine; electromagnetic induction in the generator produces electricity.

Overall efficiency is only about 33%, because the steam cycle is limited by thermodynamics (η = 1 − Tc/Th). A fusion plant would use exactly the same back end: its 14.1 MeV neutrons heat a lithium blanket, which raises steam. Fusion and fission differ at the nucleus, not at the turbine.

Evidence & its limits

Weighing the evidence: safety, emissions and uncertainty

Limitations of this data (read before trusting the numbers)

  • Nuclear death tolls are model-dependent. Long-term Chernobyl estimates range from about 4,000 (WHO, 2005) to 30,000–60,000 (TORCH, 2006). The gap exists because low-dose radiation harm is extrapolated using the contested linear no-threshold model, and the data cannot currently tell these estimates apart.
  • Lifecycle CO₂ ranges span a factor of 30 for nuclear (3.7–110 g/kWh), depending on assumptions about enrichment energy and ore grade. The single median figure hides genuine disagreement between methods.
  • Every fusion figure on these slides is a projection. There is no operational fusion power plant, so cost, safety and waste claims rest on models and single experiments (e.g. one net-gain shot at NIF), not statistics. Extraordinary care is needed comparing a 60-year operating record against a forecast.
  • 10,000-year waste containment is untestable: the safety of a geological repository rests on modelling, not on observation.

Beyond the physics

The factors that actually decide it

Economic

Fission plants are cheap to run but brutally expensive to build: Hinkley Point C (UK) has blown out past £40 billion. CSIRO's GenCost report finds new nuclear would be the most expensive new electricity for Australia, versus firmed renewables (CSIRO/AEMO, 2024). Fusion is a research expense: ITER has cost over US$22 billion before producing any power.

Legal

Nuclear power generation is prohibited in Australia by federal law, the EPBC Act 1999 (s140A) and ARPANS Act 1998 (Cth of Australia, 1999). Internationally, the Non-Proliferation Treaty and IAEA safeguards exist precisely because fission physics (slide 9) creates weapons material. Fusion currently sits outside most of this regime.

Political

Energy security drives policy. The 2022 gas crisis pushed several countries back toward fission, while Germany completed its phase-out in 2023 in response to public pressure after Fukushima. Nuclear power was also a central, unresolved issue in Australia's 2025 federal election, so this exact question is live national politics.

Sociocultural

Chernobyl (1986) and Fukushima (2011) reshaped public risk perception far beyond their measured death tolls. Public dread of radiation tends to outweigh the statistics (slide 12). In Australia, uranium mining and proposed waste sites also intersect with First Nations land rights, as seen with the abandoned Kimba waste facility (2023).

Ethical

Fission waste imposes a burden on people living hundreds of generations from now, who get no benefit and no vote. But is it ethical for wealthy nations to reject a proven low-carbon source while emissions keep warming the planet? Both stances claim the moral high ground, and the evidence on slide 12 supports parts of each.

Why this shapes viability

"Viable" is about more than physics. Fusion's physics advantages (slides 5–6, 9) have not made it viable, because the engineering has not caught up. Fission's physics is proven, yet law, cost and public trust still block it in many countries. A genuinely viable power source has to clear every one of these hurdles at once.

Summary statement

Our stance

Fission is the only currently viable nuclear power source, and should keep operating as a low-carbon bridge where it is already established, while fusion research deserves sustained investment as the likely long-term source. For Australia specifically, new fission doesn't clear the economic or legal bar; renewables plus fusion research is the defensible path.

Why we hold this view

  • Fission works now: around 440 reactors supply 9% of world electricity, and the data show it is among the safest (0.03 deaths/TWh) and cleanest (median 12 g CO₂/kWh) sources ever operated (slides 11–12).
  • Fusion is superior physics but not yet an energy source: ~4× the energy per kg, no chain-reaction risk, no millennia-scale waste, near-unlimited deuterium (slides 5–6, 9, 11), but it has produced net energy in exactly one laboratory experiment and zero watts of electricity.
  • For Australia: GenCost's economics (2024), the s140A legal ban, and our abundant renewable resources together make new fission the slower, costlier route to decarbonising (slide 13).

Accounting for other perspectives

  • "Phase out fission now" (e.g. Germany's position) takes waste ethics and accident dread seriously, but the safety data (slide 12) show replacing fission with fossil fuels costs far more lives; Germany's emissions rose after its phase-out.
  • "Go all-in on fission, everywhere" is stronger where grids lack renewable resources, and our stance allows exactly that. In Australia, though, it runs into cost data and law, not just opinion.
  • "Fusion is a distraction" has a point: it's decades away and unproven at scale. We still fund it because its fuel availability and waste profile (slides 6, 8) are the only known endgame for centuries-scale energy.

Our verdict follows the binding-energy curve itself: fission is the energy we can harvest today; fusion is the larger prize we haven't yet reached.

Limitations of this model

Like every model, this simulation trades accuracy for clarity. It is 2-D, uses about 100 nuclei instead of ~10²⁴, exaggerates collision sizes, and replaces true reaction cross-sections with simplified probabilities (slow neutrons always fission U-235 here; in reality the probability is high but not certain). The fragment tracks, γ rays and neutrino emission are decorative rather than to scale, and the fusion mode treats temperature as a single speed rather than a Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution. Its job is to show the mechanisms, namely chain reactions, criticality, breeding, and the Coulomb barrier, not to give reactor-grade numbers.
